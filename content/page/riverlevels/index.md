After the last major flood, some questions came up:

1. how deep was the flooding?
2. when do we need to put our flood barriers up?
3. how will we know how bad things are getting if it happens at night?

> [!TIP] Although we can answer these questions, we think even better answers come from two things: local data and local alerting.

The Environment Agency has **[river level monitoring stations](https://check-for-flooding.service.gov.uk/river-and-sea-levels/crudwell-malmesbury-wiltshire)** with the nearest 4km downstream at Oaksey. Of note however, several watercourses join the Swill Brook between Crudwell and Oaksey, and the depth at the EA monitoring station lags behind the depth here in Crudwell by several hours.

How is a resident of any rural parish supposed to know that `1.10m` river depth at an EA monitoring station means flooding is imminent in Crudwell? It's a useful source of data, but we would like more accuracy and confidence than this.

We think all communities might benefit from knowing: what river depth means flooding? If you break that down a bit - what river depth means flooding on a road, or in a garden, or in fields, or in a house, in different places in different conditions? We think more sensors, more local knowledge, and more analytics might be able to answer this. We also think that with more local sensor data, better alerting might be possible.

We have begun building a River Levels website which displays river depths from multiple local sensors. We think we can develop this into a simple tool that helps communities know river and flood depth on a local or even hyperlocal basis.
