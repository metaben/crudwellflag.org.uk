After the last major flood, some questions came up:

1. how deep was the flooding?
2. when do we need to put our flood barriers up?
3. how will we know how bad things are getting if it happens at night?

Our short-term fix was to persuade the Parish Council to buy and install a depth board in the river, which they have kindly done. Anybody can see the board from the river bank, although it's not a lot of fun walking out there when it's raining. In a flood event, it won't be safe to go and look at this board.

The Environment Agency has **[river level monitoring stations](https://check-for-flooding.service.gov.uk/river-and-sea-levels/crudwell-malmesbury-wiltshire)** with the nearest 4km downstream at Oaksey. Several watercourses join the Swill Brook between Crudwell and Oaksey. The depth shown by the EA monitoring station lags several hours behind the actual depth here in Crudwell.

How is a resident of a village supposed to know that (for example) `1.10m` depth downstream at the EA's monitoring station means flooding is imminent in Crudwell? It's a useful source of data, but we would like more accuracy and confidence than this is in our immediate local area.

One thing we think all communities might benefit from knowing, is what river depth means flooding? If you break it down a bit - what river depth means flooding in one place, or in a garden at another place? We think more sensors and more local knowledge can answer this. And with that sensor data, better alerting might be possible.

We have begun building a River Levels website which displays river depths from multiple local sensors. We think we can develop this into a simple tool that helps communities know river and flood depth on a local or even hyperlocal basis.

**[Click to visit riverlevels.org.uk](https://water.wmchubb.co.uk)**

This is a work in progress - do please send us any feedback or ideas.
