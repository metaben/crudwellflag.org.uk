After the last major flood, some questions came up:

1. how deep was the flooding?
2. when do we need to put our flood barriers up?
3. how will we know how bad things are if the water is rising at night?

> [!TIP] Although we can broadly answer these questions using a weather forecast and Environment Agency warnings, we think even better accuracy and confidence comes from two things: local data and local alerting.

## Environment Agency monitoring

The Environment Agency has **[river level monitoring stations](https://check-for-flooding.service.gov.uk/river-and-sea-levels/crudwell-malmesbury-wiltshire)** with the nearest 4km downstream at Oaksey. Of note however, several watercourses join the Swill Brook between Crudwell and Oaksey, and the depth at the EA monitoring station lags behind the depth here in Crudwell by several hours.

How is a resident of any rural parish supposed to know that `1.10m` river depth at an EA monitoring station means flooding is imminent in Crudwell? It's a useful source of data, but we would like more accuracy and confidence than this.

## Local monitoring

We think all communities might benefit from knowing: at what river depth will there be flooding?

There is a lot of variety in how flooding occurs - think about roads, fields, driveways and gardens, and water coming off the road into homes. National tools are useful, but they don't help residents in rural areas know this. We think more sensors, more local knowledge, and more analytics might be able to give better answers. We also think better alerting is possible.

We've now installed our first river level monitoring in Crudwell, as an experiment. We think we can do work that complements what the EA do with their own funding.

## New River Levels website

We have begun building a River Levels website to display river depths from multiple local sensors. We think we can develop this into a simple tool to help communities know river and flood depth on a local or hyperlocal basis.
